The first 48 hours after receiving an audit notice set the tone for everything that follows. Whether you're an individual taxpayer or a corporate entity, the steps you take — and the mistakes you avoid — can materially affect the outcome.

## Step 1: Don't Panic, But Don't Delay

An audit notice triggers specific statutory deadlines. In most jurisdictions, you have a fixed period (often 30 days) to respond. Missing that window can result in a default assessment. Your immediate priority is to:

- Note all deadlines on the notice
- Determine the scope of the audit (which tax years, which issues)
- Assess whether the audit is civil or has potential criminal dimensions

## Step 2: Engage Counsel Immediately

If the audit involves material amounts or sensitive issues, engage experienced tax controversy counsel before speaking with the revenue agent. Attorney-client privilege only attaches to communications with your lawyer — not your accountant. Under the *Kovel* doctrine (in the U.S.), a lawyer can engage an accountant as part of the legal team in a way that extends privilege to communications, but this must be structured properly from the outset.

## Step 3: Preserve — But Don't Create — Documents

Document destruction after receiving an audit notice can constitute obstruction. However, you are under no obligation to create new documents that the tax authority hasn't specifically requested. Be strategic:

- Issue a litigation hold notice internally
- Preserve all relevant records, including electronic communications
- Do not prepare new analyses or "defense memos" without counsel's guidance — these may be discoverable

## Step 4: Prepare for the Initial Conference

The revenue agent will typically request an initial meeting. This is your opportunity to:

- Understand the agent's specific concerns
- Propose a reasonable timeline for document production
- Establish a professional, cooperative tone — without volunteering information beyond what's requested

## Step 5: Build Your Substantive Defense Early

Don't wait until the revenue agent proposes adjustments. Begin assembling your legal and factual defenses immediately. This includes:

- Identifying the governing statutory provisions and any applicable administrative guidance
- Gathering comparable transaction data to support your positions
- Evaluating potential penalty defenses (reasonable cause, good faith reliance on professional advice, etc.)

## A Long Game

Tax audits are marathons, not sprints. The average field audit takes 12–18 months from opening conference to final determination. Patience, preparation, and professional representation are your most valuable assets.
