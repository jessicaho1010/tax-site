Recent IRS enforcement data points to a significant shift in how offshore non-compliance is being penalised. For years, the trend was toward ever-increasing penalties — but 2026 is telling a more nuanced story.

## The Numbers Behind the Shift

IRS data through the first quarter of 2026 shows a notable decline in the average FBAR penalty amount per case compared to the same period in 2024. At the same time, the total number of cases opened has increased. This suggests the IRS is pursuing more cases but imposing more proportionate penalties.

## What's Driving the Change?

Several factors appear to be at work:

- **Judicial pushback** — Several federal courts have expressed discomfort with the IRS stacking multiple non-willful penalties across accounts, leading to more restrained penalty calculations in exam.
- **Congressional pressure** — Bipartisan legislation introduced in late 2025 (the *Offshore Penalty Fairness Act*) proposes capping non-willful FBAR penalties, and the IRS may be adjusting in anticipation.
- **Improved disclosure rates** — Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures and the Voluntary Disclosure Practice continue to bring taxpayers into compliance, reducing the population of truly non-compliant filers.

## What Cross-Border Taxpayers Should Do Now

If you have unreported foreign accounts or income, the landscape is still serious — but it may be more navigable than it was a few years ago. Here are practical steps:

1. **Assess your exposure.** Determine which jurisdictions are involved, what reporting obligations exist (FBAR, FATCA Form 8938, PFIC Forms 8621, etc.), and what years remain open.
2. **Consider voluntary disclosure options.** The IRS still prioritises voluntary compliance, and coming forward before you're contacted generally leads to better outcomes.
3. **Don't assume you're invisible.** FATCA IGA reporting by foreign financial institutions means the IRS already has data on many offshore accounts. The question isn't whether they can find you — it's when.

## The Bottom Line

The enforcement environment is evolving, and 2026 trends suggest the IRS is calibrating its approach. However, the core principle hasn't changed: proactive compliance is always cheaper than reactive defense.
